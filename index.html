Multimodality and the Research Process

Demonstrated through Stargate SG-1

ANTH2610 — Language and Culture  ·  University of Idaho  ·  Connor Wolfe

Living Language  ·  Laura M. Ahearn

What This Project Is About

Rather than writing a traditional paper, I'm making a web paper because its supposed to be easier for embedding media and showing examples. The paper is structured around two core concepts from of Ahearn's Living Language: multimodality and participant observation. The goal is to show how these concepts actually work in practice, using specific scenes from the TV show.

Chapter 2 — Gestures, Sign Languages, and Multimodality

In Chapter 2, Ahearn writes about how communication is multimodal. Meaning it can happen more than just with Speech. People can communicate using gesture, facial expressions, body language, with object around them, etc.

Chapter 3 — The Research Process in Linguistic Anthropology

In Chapter 3, Ahearn writes about how linguistic anthropologists actually do their research. The most widely used method is called Participant Observation. Where researchers will spend months or years in a community immersing themselves in the Language or culture.

What Is Stargate SG-1?

The Show

Stargate SG-1 is a science fiction series that ran from 1997 to 2007. Its about a team of explorers that go to different planets via a “Stargate” (big wormhole device). The team is made up of mostly military, but also some civilian, people. During their Adventures the team meets many different Alien Cultures and societies. Which I think makes it a great match for this class.

Daniel Jackson

Dr.Daniel Jackson is the team archaeologist and linguist. He serves as the team Cultural expert and is often found doing pretty much exactly Participant Observation. So he makes a great example of what anthropologists might do in the field.

The Episode — "The First Ones" (S4E8, 1999)

The Episode I picked out for this paper is “The First Ones”, where Daniel gets captured by an Unas (the specifics aren’t important, big alien guy) The episode follows Daniel as he tries to reason with this Unas (we find out his name is Chaka), but they don’t share a language so he struggles with this throughout the episode. Until eventually cracking the language roughly halfway through while eating. . . lets say food.

Body Language and Gesture as Communication

Multimodality in Action

The clip below is the best example of multimodality in the episode. Daniel doesn’t speak the Unas’ language yet so he can’t ask for water. He can’t negotiate or explain anything yet. So, he uses loud sounds (“Hey!”) and gestures (like when he gestures for the water) to communicate his needs. What’s interesting is that this behavior is reciprocated by the Unas. Like, while trying to drink it the Unas sees him doing it in an unsafe way and so uses body language to communicate how one should be drinking from a lake.

This is a great example of what Ahern is talking about with multimodality. Both of them are talking without using any words. Just sounds and gestures. This demonstrates to me Ahearn’s Myth 1 “Sign Languages are not ‘real’ languages”. Though what their doing is not exactly sign language I think its close enough to demonstrate the example. Daniel and the Unas are talking, just using gestures and related sounds.

Adjacency Pairs in Communication

Using Food and Eating for Bonding

The clip below shows another good example of multimodality, and a little bit of the field work paying off (where he can speak the language a little now), where Daniel communicates with the Unas through eating food. What is interesting with this clip is that Daniel uses the food to communicate, and bond, with the Unas. Through sharing the their food with each other, they are able to create a bond that they could not throughout the whole previous of the episode

I think this shows a potentially interesting case of Adjacency Pairs. Where The eating of food sort of creates the response of “oh this is safe”, or the feeling of the moment being a bonding one sort of. And how tossing food at on another signals to eat that food, and how throwing the food away creates a sort of upset response. Even though there’s no words being used I think it shows Adjacency pairs in action.

Daniel as a Field Researcher/Anthropologist

Participant Observation Without Meaning To

Throughout the Episode Daniel doesn’t just try to survive his capture with the Unas. He observes and tries to become familiar with the Unas’ culture norms and language. In a way very similar to how I imagine Anthropologists in the field do (although I imagine not being capture to do this is peferable).

This is best seen throughout the whole episode rather than in a single clip — Daniel's recording narration happens at several points, particularly in the early portions of the episode. If your interested in seeing this then I highly encourage you to watch S4E8 in full, it's on Amazon Prime Video.

Conclusion

What the Show Gets Right

I want to recognize that this is a tv show and is not real. So the examples will not be 100 percent accurate. BUT, I think these writers, and the actors for that matter, did a really good job showing these concepts in this episode (the show as a whole really for that matter, it’s a really good show).

So, I think “The First Ones” serves as a great sort of case study for these concepts. Because it shows how they work out in a high pressure situation. Using not just speech but various different gestures, sounds, objects, etc. Daniel was able to communicate with another species. This really shows Multimodality in action. And the episode shows how Anthropologists might interact with people who don’t speak a lick of the same language. Maybe even how they interact with hostile group like isolated tribes.
